Conceptual metaphor theory. The most obvious feature that attracts attention is the way information is presented in OKM. Papers are grouped into bubbles of similar content and the user can further explore individual bubbles to see more details. In of their proposals the authors argue that this visualization comes with cognitive benefits as the mental workload is kept to reasonable size by leveraging different levels of abstraction. While this approach has been user tested in Kraker et al. (2015) I believe that the the true benefit in terms of cognition is due to the metaphoric structure of these visualizations. It is possible to break down the interface using Lakoff and Johnson’s conceptual metaphor theory. We usually think of research disciplines and fields as container metaphors (e.g., “to be IN the social sciences”) and similarly the bubbles serve as containers for specific papers. The users can literally click and dive into a bubble or research field to explore the elements within. Several orientational metaphors are at work when the proximity of bubbles are indicating the topical relatedness of bubbles and the introduction of layers (the authors are proposing nested layers of topics as a future feature) also fit our natural understanding of research fields as sub- and supercategories. Finally, another very powerful structural metaphor is already pointed out by the creators and the name of the tool, namely that of maps and cartography. Humans use maps to navigate space; they can either serve to orientate one in both new and familiar regions and by varying the amount of detail a map can either give an overview of a continent or meticulously describe the alleys of Palermo. Furthermore, it is interesting to point out that the maps produced by OKM are not to be understood as static, objectively true snapshots of the world. While the common understanding and usage of cartographic maps might rely on such an assumption (navigating the seas was a daunting, if not impossible, task before the introduction of the mercator projection which finally allowed for the p angles and directions) it is important to remember that every two dimensional map is a projection that introduces distortions. If the cartographic metaphor is be taken serious, knowledge maps will take on various projections of concepts (currently 100 most relevant concepts are condensed based on their metadata and abstracts) depending on the purpose of the user.
